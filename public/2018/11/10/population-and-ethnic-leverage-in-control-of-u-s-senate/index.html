Each state, then as now, elects two Senators, whether it accounts for 2% of the national population, in the case of Delaware, or 22%, for Virginia.1 Within each state, those potentially eligible to vote represented around 15% of the in-state population, in the slave holding states, and around 25% in the states with low slave population.2 The fundamental feature of the composition of the Senate, equal representation of states rather than population, or even voters, was an integral part of the design of the Constitution.3.

The Senate is a non-democratic institution when considered at the national level. One historian virtually makes the case that the Senate represented nothing other than itself until 1957.4

Four states, Virginia, Massachusetts, Pennsylvania and North Carolina accounted for 55% of the national population residing in states but only 31% of the members of the Senate. Voting power was not evenly divided between the major slaveholding states, which included Virginia, North Carolina, Maryland, South Carolina and Georgia, and the remaining states.

The non-democratic nature of this arrangement is highlighted by the ratio of voters in Delaware, the smallest state in 1790, and Virginia, the largest. Given their relative number of voters, each Delaware vote had 10.7 times the influence on the factional composition of the Senate compared to each Virginia vote. Based on the total population, including the majority of dis-enfranchised, Delaware influence was slightly higher, 13.9 times that of Virginia.

While lowest-population Delaware enjoyed a relative voting power 13.9 times that of Virginia, the most populous state in 1790, the relative adjantage of the eight states comprising today’s cumulative 2% of the population, relative to 2017’s most populuous state, California, has declined to 5.95. However, today’s lowest population state, Wyoming, has a population based relative advantage multiple of 68.2 over California. Simply put, it takes 68 California residents to have the same influence on the selection of a Senator as 1 Wyoming resident.

The 20 states with populations of less than approximately 3.6 million, taken together, have the approximately the same population as California. It takes 20 California residents to have the same influence on the selection of a single Senator as 1 resident of those 20 states taken as a whole.

Just nine states: California, Texas, Florida, New York, Pennslyvania, Illinois, Ohio, Georgia and North Carolina comprise the majority of the population of the United States, approximately 52%. The remaining 48% of the population live in states with populations less than 10 million.

Not only is population unevenly distributed, so is economic productivity, measured on per capita basis.5.

The Resulting Political Instability

An arrangement in which 17.5% of the population can, if they agree or if dissenters can be replaced by other states, block legislation by the Congress is inherently unstable; a minority colalition of the 41 smallest states, 48.8% of the population, has unfettered power over the majority of the national population, 52% of the nine largest states.

The representative democracy model based on the 1790 model of indirect, free white male enfranchisement no longer serves as either a division of power contemplated when the Constitution was adopted in 1787, nor as a model that facilitates changes, preserves neeeded measures, dampens volatility and promotes confidence in risk taking in the face of unpredictable legal changes, or prevents the money and soft influence of special interests from gaming the decisions of the Senate. This part of the Constitution is failing, overlooked, and a danger to both principles of federalism and governing institutions that reflect the mediated will of the people. How to fix it is a debate that needs to begin.

Proposed

What is a simple way to measure the potential of a minority of the population of the contemporary United States to elect the party in control the United States Senate? Similarly, how can the potential of those who are white, non-Hispanic to control the Senate be measured? My friend and former colleague Todd Baker suggested this topic.

In this essay, I propose four measures.

  1. SLI, the state leverage index based on population alone
  2. WLI, the white leverage index based on population alone
  3. \(TWLI\ =\ SLI\ *\ WLI\)
  4. SPI, the state polarity index, based on the proportional vote of the winning candidate, averaged over the 2014, 2016 and 2018 elections.

Based on the 2017 Census estimates the SLI and WLI are the simple ratios

\(index = 1/(sub\_population/total\_population)\)


The third index is simply the product of the first two, which I call the Total White Leverage Index, TWLI.

The SPI is the weighted mean of the vote proportion received by the winning candidate in the 2014, 2016, and 2018 elections.[^Two two independent Senators, who caucus with the Democratic party were classified as Democrats.] To distinguish between parties, The Democratic winning candidates are expressed as a negative proportion; for example a Democratic candidate winning 54.1% of a vote is indexed as -0.541.6

To illustrate, assume that the total population of the United States is 325,000,000 and a small state has a population of 700,000, of whom 600,000 are white, non-Hispanic.7.

The SLI for the state would be 464.29 and its WLI would be 1.17. The TWLI would be 541.67.

The interpretation of SLI is that high scores indicate more potential influence on the composition of the Senate compared to an arrangement where Senate seats were allocated proportionally to national population. The interpretation of WLI is that high scores indicate more potential influence on the selection of a Senator in a given state held by white, non-Hispanic voters. The interpretation of TWLI combines the two measures, and high scores indicate more potential influence on the composition of the Senate by white, non-Hispanic voters compared to an arrangement where Senate seats were allocated proportionally to population.

Limitations of the Base Model

Not all white, non-Hispanic voters vote alike, even within a state. We know that the proportion of each state’s population that is registered to vote or ineligible to vote (age, criminal history, citizenship status) varies. We know that propensity to vote varies. We know that it is possible to encourage or discourage voting within state based on precincts and the ways in which elections are administered.

The base model takes none of these real-world facts into account. The indices are intended solely to gauge potential for control of the Senate by a portion of the national population. It is merely a starting point from which additional variables can be added. Like all models, the base model derives its usefulness from simplifying reality.

Conclusions

White, non-Hispanic voters in the group of states constituting the minority of the total US population, have the potential to create 22 Senate seats for Republicans based on recent history based on their WLI and polarity. The remaining minority states have the potential to create 20 Senate seats for Democrats on the same basis. The remaining 20 seats controlled by minority states are insufficient to create control of the Senate without increasing WLI, polarity or both in the absence of 8-9 (depending on the control of the Presidency) majority states.

In otherwords, under current conditions and the assumptions in this analysis, the Senate will be closely divided. Neither party will achieve cloture or impeachment level control. The Senate is divided into three classes; different groups states vote for Senate each year. Demographic changes may affect WLI, as relative composition of incoming voters changes through natural increase or decrease and net migration, the composition of outgoing voters change as a function of migration or death, propensitity to vote changes, and polarity changes. None of these dynamic factors are included in this essay.

Appendix

Disclosure

I am not a political scientist and I have not reviewed the literature. My training is as a lawyer and an applied statistician (also known as a data scientist.) The purpose of this essay is to provide a beginning basis for assessment of future control frameworks of the U.S. Senate. It is only one potential beginning point. The question of how to assure inclusion of all perspectives on matters of national interest vs. matters of predominanty state interest is a central problem of how we should govern ourselves. Small states deserve a voice and large states require a counterbalance. We have managed, through extra-constitutional means, such as campaign finance and interest group liberalism in the Altshuler sense, to introduce a private sector factor into the governance equation in the Senate that is neither democratic nor representative. This should concern partisans of all degree.
